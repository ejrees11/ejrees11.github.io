Dr. Jackie Cha

ISYE 552- Dr. Cha came to us with a wealth of regulatory and medical knowledge. I was interested to learn that 'medical devices' technically pertains to all kinds of things, including tongue depressors, microwaves, and prosthetics. Dr. Cha also got my attention by mentioning what a significant percentage, over 60%, of medical device adverse events occur just because the user is unaware how to fully operate the device, classified as a 'use error'. In a time when I have heard some people say that human factors is a dying field of work, it was relieving to hear that human factors study and clearence is required of all medical devices above a certain class to gain FDA approval and go to market. I learned that the FDA requires 15 users per study group, but that 85% of problems are identified after just 5. We also did an exercise regarding grouping different logos, which taught the fact that everyone can have different interpretations of groupings, icons, and just generally whatever they are looking at. The main takeaway that I understood from Dr. Cha, though, was that use of a device should never rely solely on training. If the item is not intuitive enough for a novice to be able to at least partially function or understand, it likely needs some redesign to get it to a better place.

Dr. Arjun Rao

ISYE 552- Our second guest speaker was Dr. Arjun Rao, speaking to us virtually from his office at Boeing. He focused more heaviy than other speakers on his personal career journey, and offered advice for every step. He noted that human factors can exist in many industries, as the problems hfe's aim to solve can exist in a variety of products. I was interested to hear his perspective on the differences between being a typical engineer and being an engineering manager, as this is something I have wondered about for my future. His strategy of designing collaboratively was refreshing to hear, as it seems many engineers get a work computer and work in a dark corner, never speaking to anyone. These collaborative designs also seemed to be much safer, as they take a array of expertises and perspectives as input. Especially in aerospace, where Dr. Rao works, he noted the importance of designing for both normal and non-normal operating conditions to ensure optimality and the best safety performance of your design. I think some of the most interesting things I earned from Dr. Rao were in regards to working in a highly competitive business, as opposed to academia. The pace and intention of his studies as a professional, he noted, are far different from thoe he used to complete on university campuses. Typical time to design and complete his work is two weeks, and he was more than willing to sacrifice some rigor if it meant he gained more influence or evidence for an upcoming decision to be made, which he never would do at an institution.

Dr. Prakriti Parijat

ISYE 552- Even though Dr. Parijat was not the speaker present to discuss triangulation, she opened discussing some of the differences and limits of physical measurements vs behavioral observations. She also discussed how some of Instacart's demand predictions have not been close to met, so the company has adapted to meet differnt demands, such as pioneering a cart that scans items automatically as they are dropped in so shoppers can move quicker. Initially, her team used a computer vision model to complete this task. But, after many complications, they adapted again and decided just to mount a scanner on the cart to identify items that shoppers were buying. Her process steps were to 1. shape strategy, 2. evaluate solutions, 3. measure impact, and 4. scale. Similar to Dr. Rao, she noted how human factors research in these companies is more of a single input than the final decision. She said her team searches for the minimum amount of evidence needed to draw a conclusion, as opposed to going deep into every element of a study with extreme rigor. I thought it was interesting how she noted that in consumer research, people's opinions often disagree with their actions. In her field of food, Dr. Parijit also noted the high amount of personal biases that people have, whether due to their upbringing, cooking abilities, or financial situation. To overcome this, she said that every study or conclusion should tell a story, and be convincing enough to pul people with you. Senior leaders in companies are most easily moved by numbers and figures, not opinions, she said, so this is what she aims to present to them. Most importantly, though, in the workplace, she said that quick research and build cycles and flexibility are the most important traits to carry.

Dr. Rohith Karthikeyan

ISYE 552- Dr. Karthikeyan came to us from Apple to discuss data triangulation, but I really enjoyed a lot of his other perspectives outside of this topic. He was an electrifying speaker, and started his presentation discussing how an individual's specific background changes how they make decisions and how everyone genuinely sees reality a little bit differently. Many studies, he said, sek to reduce the impact of any preexisting bias, but that these biases are actually important enviornmental adaptations that can be adventageous to an individual. As far as traingulation, he noted it as a neccesity, as no single measurement type will ever see the entire system. He said "Don't solve a human, solve the experience.", which I found to be an interesting and important persective. Human factors research, he said, never measures the actual experience itself, but measures a variety of proxies to draw conclusions to a real interction. Subjective, objective, and observational inputs all must come together to fully inform any decision or reccomendation a study makes. He said to think of your own perspective as a tool, especially in the job market or in any sort of design role. As you triangulate, he said to view every no as a move towards refinement, as opposed to an idea being killed. He advised that data will always e around, but the framework for making decisions with it will not be, so that is the realm to practice and focus on as we all advance our careers.
