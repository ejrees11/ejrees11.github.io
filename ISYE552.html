Dr. Jackie Cha

ISYE 552- Dr. Cha came to us with a wealth of regulatory and medical knowledge. I was interested to learn that 'medical devices' technically pertains to all kinds of things, including tongue depressors, microwaves, and prosthetics. Dr. Cha also got my attention by mentioning what a significant percentage, over 60%, of medical device adverse events occur just because the user is unaware how to fully operate the device, classified as a 'use error'. In a time when I have heard some people say that human factors is a dying field of work, it was relieving to hear that human factors study and clearence is required of all medical devices above a certain class to gain FDA approval and go to market. I learned that the FDA requires 15 users per study group, but that 85% of problems are identified after just 5. We also did an exercise regarding grouping different logos, which taught the fact that everyone can have different interpretations of groupings, icons, and just generally whatever they are looking at. The main takeaway that I understood from Dr. Cha, though, was that use of a device should never rely solely on training. If the item is not intuitive enough for a novice to be able to at least partially function or understand, it likely needs some redesign to get it to a better place.
